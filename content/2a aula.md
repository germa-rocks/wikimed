## Pressures in the Cardiovascular System
We talked about pressure in the respiratory system in Chapter 1. It's pretty analogous in the circulatory system. There are different kinds of pressure, and this chapter is going to focus on the dynamic and static pressures of the circulatory system, just like we talked about the dynamic and static pressures of the respiratory system. 

The dynamic pressure of the circulatory system drives blood flow, just like gas flow in the respiratory system. The relationship between this pressure and flow is known as **resistance**, so we're going to talk about that as well. And the static pressure of the circulatory system is responsible for vascular or ventricular volume, just as in the respiratory system it was a determinant of thoracic volume. So this should be a lot of a review for you if you've gone through Chapter 1. The relationship between the static pressure (or the transmural pressure) of the circulatory system and vascular volume or ventricular volume is known as **compliance**.

### The Idealized Blood Vessel
This is my idealized blood vessel: the perfect non-distensible tin pipe that we use to model the circulatory system, which as I'm sure all of you appreciate, the circulatory system is almost nothing like that. 

The dynamic pressure generates a flow—in this case, it's blood flow. So there's an upstream pressure, a downstream pressure, and this drives a flow. The relationship between pressure and flow here is resistance. 

Similarly, the static pressure determines a volume. The static pressure of the cardiovascular system is known as the **transmural pressure**, and it relates to volume by compliance. As you can see, the pressure in the upstream portion of the vessel is actually greater, and so the transmural pressure is a little bit greater than the pressure down here, because you lose some of this pressure with the resistance to the flow. So in effect, the transmural pressure in the proximal portion of a blood vessel actually tends to be greater. As a result, instead of being this perfect non-distensible vessel of unchanging caliber, blood vessels actually don't really behave like this at all. They can actually be tapered, where the upstream pressure and the radius of the vessel is actually greater than the downstream pressure. 

As you can appreciate, the greater radius upstream than downstream will actually result in greater flows upstream because as the radius increases, the resistance decreases. And so the pressure-flow relationship in blood vessels, just as it was in the respiratory system, is actually not quite linear, and that is to be discussed.

## Systemic Venous Return
Systemic venous return is very important. It's the vascular physiology half of cardiovascular physiology. We live in a very cardiocentric world, and we often think of the cardiovascular system only in terms of the Frank-Starling mechanism, but if you think of it only in terms of the Frank-Starling mechanism and not as well in terms of venous return, you're really missing half of the picture.

Over time, **Venous Return equals the blood ejected from the heart**, that is, inflow is equal to outflow, and so they can be considered equivalent. Venous return and cardiac output is a flow (in liters per minute), and much like airflow in the lungs, pressure and flow can be plotted against each other. This relationship is the resistance. But in order to understand venous return, you have to understand both the upstream pressure and the downstream pressure, because like anything where there's flow, you need an upstream pressure and a downstream pressure (i.e., a pressure differential), just like in weather (wind outside needs a high pressure and a low pressure). This is typically represented on a graph.

### The Venous Return Curve
This is your Venous Return curve. On the y-axis, you have blood flow in liters per minute, and on the x-axis, you have pressure. Unlike the Campbell diagram, this is a vascular pressure, which we historically measure in millimeters of mercury. We're going to really get into the nitty-gritty of this curve because it's important to understand cardiovascular physiology and venous return.

**Right Atrial Pressure**
The right atrial pressure is the most commonly assessed pressure, both invasively and non-invasively, of the circulatory system. As I'm sure you all know who've been in the ICU, we put in a right IJ, left IJ, or a subclavian line (a central line into the superior vena cava) and we measure the central venous pressure, which is essentially exactly equivalent to your right atrial pressure barring any wild caval anatomic abnormalities. You've absolutely got to understand how the right atrial pressure is determined. It's frightening how many people do not fully understand the physiology of the right atrial pressure and then use it to drive management decisions, which frankly can be harmful at times.

The right atrial pressure is the downstream pressure for venous return. As right atrial pressure is decreased, venous return will increase, and that makes sense—if it's the downstream pressure and you lower it, you're widening that pressure differential and therefore flow will increase. 

From this, you can generate a curve defined in terms of:
*   **Upstream pressure:** The mean systemic pressure.
*   **Downstream pressure:** The right atrial pressure (the independent variable, what we're altering).
*   **Venous Return:** The dependent variable on the y-axis (flow back to the right heart).

### Mean Systemic Filling Pressure (PMS) & Resistance
The venous return curve is determined by three variables:
1.  **Mean Systemic Filling Pressure:** This is the point on the x-axis (the x-intercept).
2.  **Right Atrial Pressure:** Also on the x-axis.
3.  **Resistance to Venous Return:** Depicted as changes in the slope of this graph.

You can see that venous return will increase if the right atrial pressure decreases. Comparing a higher right atrial pressure (lower venous return) to a lower right atrial pressure, you get a greater venous return and therefore greater blood flow to the heart. 

Venous return will increase with a decrease in resistance to venous return for the same right atrial pressure. A graphical representation of a decreased resistance to venous return shows the mean systemic filling pressure is the same, but the slope of the line has changed. You can compare the same right atrial pressures, but the venous return (flow to the heart) is actually greater for the slope that represents a decreased resistance to venous return.

**Changing Mean Systemic Filling Pressure**
The Mean Systemic Filling Pressure can be changed as well, shifting the curve rightwards and leftwards. For an increased mean systemic filling pressure, you can see that for the same right atrial pressure, you have a greater venous return.

What is the mean systemic pressure? It was that x-intercept. This is the pressure in the vasculature when the venous return (cardiac output) is zero. If I were to arrest the circulation—make everything stop and let all the pressures throughout the arteries, arterioles, capillaries, venules, veins, and all four chambers of the heart come to an equilibration—and then measure the right atrial pressure, it's going to equal this mean systemic filling pressure. 

If I were to restart the heart (jump start it, give it a little bit of electrical shock), the heart starts moving blood from the venous system to the arterial system. The stroke volume (cardiac output) starts being transferred to the arterial system. Then right atrial pressure is lowered and venous return increases, but the mean systemic pressure stays just about the same as when the circulation is arrested. That's because the venous capacitance beds are large. 70% of your blood at any one time is in the venous capacitance beds. You kind of have to think of it as a big swimming pool where there's so much volume and so much compliance that whether flow is arrested or begins again as a result of cardiac function, the pressure in the venous capacitance beds is essentially the same.

The mean systemic pressure (abbreviated as PMS) is essentially determined by two variables via the **compliance equation**:
*   *Compliance = Change in Volume / Change in Pressure*
*   Rearranged: *Change in Pressure = Change in Volume / Compliance*

The volume here is the **stressed volume**, and the compliance is the **vascular compliance**. The increase in mean systemic pressure that occurs in response to a given change in stressed volume is the static pressure, derived from the compliance equation. Let's dig into what stressed and unstressed volumes mean. 

### Stressed vs. Unstressed Volume
This is a crucial physiological phenomenon to understand if you want to understand venous return and right atrial pressure. 

Think about blowing up a balloon or a beach ball. You have to add a certain amount of volume with essentially no change in pressure or stretch on the wall of the balloon. This is your **unstressed volume** (the flattened vein). If you increase volume with very little or no change in pressure, that's just filling up the vessel. But then you get to a certain volume, and pressure then begins to rise. You've started to tense the walls of the vein, and the pressure in the walls is going up. This is the **stressed volume**. 

The total volume of this vein is always equal to the stressed volume and the unstressed volume. You can shift this around a little bit:
*   If you want to increase the stressed volume, you can either **increase the total volume** in the veins (like giving a fluid bolus). The pressure has therefore gone up (your mean systemic pressure goes up).
*   Or, you can **decrease the fraction of the unstressed volume**. If you change the characteristics of the blood vessel—keeping the volume the same but decreasing the fraction of the unstressed volume—any additional volume becomes stressed volume. This will increase your stressed volume and increase your mean systemic pressure.

### Capacitance vs. Compliance
I commonly get the question: what's the difference between capacitance and compliance? You'll read these physiology papers and read about them. What really is the difference? 
*   **Capacitance:** Refers to a *total* volume for a *total* pressure.
*   **Compliance:** Is more dynamic; it's the *change* in volume for a *change* in pressure (a derivative, referring to the slope of the curve). 

If you consider two blood vessels, the slopes at all points might be essentially the same—meaning the compliance (the derivative of the curve) is the same value. But the capacitance can be lower in one. For the same volume, the pressure in one vessel is much higher, so it has a lower capacitance, although its compliance may actually be quite the same. It's a subtle but important distinction.

**Circuit Function Shifts**
As I just said, you can increase the mean systemic pressure by either:
1.  Increasing the total volume.
2.  Decreasing the unstressed volume fraction.
3.  Decreasing the vascular compliance.

This results in an increase in the upstream pressure and therefore a greater venous return for any right atrial pressure. Conversely, a decrease in blood volume, an increase in unstressed volume, or an increase in vascular compliance will shift the venous return curve to the left in a parallel fashion. 

I'll refer to a rightward shift in the venous return curve as an **"increase in circuit function"** and a leftward shift as a **"decrease in circuit function."** I'm using these terms (stealing them from Sheldon Magder) as an analogy to cardiac function (increase or decrease in cardiac function). 

Common causes for an *increase* in circuit function (right shift):
*   Volume expansion (filling up with liters of crystalloid).
*   Adrenergic stimulus that decreases the unstressed volume of the veins (adrenergic agents). This is a response to hypovolemia as well (shifting your curve rightward as a response to adrenergic tone).

Common causes for a *decrease* in circuit function (left shift):
*   Volume loss (hemorrhage).
*   Sedatives/anti-adrenergic agents. When patients are about to get intubated and you slug them with sedation, you decrease their mean systemic pressure. With the initiation of positive intrathoracic pressure (increasing right atrial pressure), these things co-conspire to cause low venous return and low cardiac output.

*Note: Circuit function depends on multiple variables, including the mean systemic pressure (which itself is determined by multiple variables) and the resistance to venous return. Volume status (how hyper, hypo, or euvolemic you are) is really the numerator here, playing one role in mean systemic pressure, which plays one role in venous return and right atrial pressure.*

### Maximal Venous Return & The Vascular Waterfall
On the venous return curve, there's a phenomenon known as **maximal venous return**. As you saw on the previous curves, there's a plateau which tends to occur in the spontaneously breathing patient at around atmospheric pressure or zero. 

The mechanism for this is very important to understand. It occurs when the vascular pressure within the IVC (or the SVC) drops below its ambient pressure. This results in a collapsing effect of these vessels. When you're taking a deep breath in, you're lowering your intrathoracic pressure (raising intra-abdominal pressure), and when right atrial pressure drops below this intra-abdominal pressure, the IVC collapses. You can see this fluttering effect, which you've probably witnessed on an ultrasound of the IVC. 

But when this phenomenon occurs, flow doesn't stop. As the IVC collapses on itself, instantaneously the pressure head then supersedes the pressure within the blood vessel and flow instantaneously begins again. This fluttering effect causes a plateau or a flow-limiting effect—it doesn't stop flow. This collapsing paradigm is known as a **Starling resistor**, and the phenomenon of maximal flow independent of downstream pressure is known as a **vascular waterfall**. This essentially sets a brake limit on cardiac output. If you lower right atrial pressure below atmospheric pressure and hit this plateau, this prevents cardiac output from increasing beyond that point. The heart can only pump out what it receives in return.

### Abdominal Circulatory Interactions
This is an introduction to abdominal circulatory interaction. If you increase your abdominal pressure (descent of the diaphragm with inspiration, or intra-abdominal hypertension), it tends to shift the venous return curve rightwards. It shifts your mean systemic pressure rightwards, giving you a greater pressure head driving venous return back to the heart. 

But as we saw, the increased intra-abdominal pressure tends to collapse the IVC and lower maximal venous return, effectively increasing the resistance to venous return. So you have a shift in the effective mean systemic pressure but with that collapse, you effectively increase resistance to venous return, dropping the slope and lowering maximal venous return. 

**West Zones of the Abdomen**
What exactly happens when you inspire? The predominant effect is determined by the underlying volume status of the patient. This was work done by Takata and colleagues in the mid-90s, applying the idea of West Zones (1, 2, and 3) in the lungs to the abdomen. 

*   **Volume Replete (West Zone 3 equivalent):** If the patient is volume replete, the abdominal pressure increases, but the alveolar (or in this case, abdominal venous) pressure exceeds the surrounding pressure. There's an increase in venous return, an increase in IVC flow, and an increase in maximal venous return.
*   **Volume Deplete (West Zone 2 equivalent):** If the patient is volume deplete and their abdominal vascular beds are predominated by Zone 2 physiology, you get a predominance of this venous resistance effect, and venous return is actually blunted in these hypovolemic patients.

### Pharmacological Effects (Adrenergic Medications)
Adrenergic medications have an effect on vascular capacitance. Giving adrenergic medications tends to shift the venous return curve rightwards. However, there are interesting pharmacological properties relating to receptor expression:
*   **Selective Alpha-stimulation:** Tends to increase the resistance to venous return. There's some thought that this primarily occurs in hepatic venous beds.
*   **Beta-stimulation:** Tends to decrease the resistance to venous return, shifting the slope of the curve up and to the right. 

The body's endogenous adrenergic response (releasing catecholamines acting on both Beta and Alpha receptors) tends to facilitate a response where you have an increase in the pressure head (mean systemic pressure) and you recruit unstressed volume into stressed volume, but at the same time, the Beta receptor stimulation tends to decrease the resistance to venous return. Conversely, solitary Alpha-adrenergic stimulation (like Neosynephrine) may have a more detrimental effect on limiting venous return by increasing the resistance. Much of this literature comes from Sheldon Magder's studies on venous return in sepsis and septic shock.

**Volume Depletion and Adrenergic Response:**
As we talked about, volume depletion shifts the venous return curve leftwards (drop in mean systemic pressure). But when you're volume deplete, you have an adrenergic response that recruits unstressed volume, compensating for volume loss by shifting the curve back rightwards. In a 70 kg man, just by adrenergic stimulation, you can recruit about 2 liters of blood (like an auto-bolus). But if patients living on their adrenergic tone are given sedatives or anti-adrenergic agents, you completely blunt this effect. The curve reverses, and these patients just plummet very quickly.

## Pulmonary Venous Return
To wrap up this first part, I'm now going to turn to pulmonary venous return. The physiology is really quite similar. 

An identical curve can be created, but the x-axis instead of right atrial pressure is now **left atrial pressure**. Like systemic venous return, the pulmonary venous return curve is shifted to the right when pulmonary mean systemic pressure increases. And in a manner similar to intra-abdominal pressure, this pulmonary venous return physiology was actually known *prior* to the abdominal venous return physiology (work by Robotham and Permutt in the 70s). The return to the left heart was known, and later applied to the systemic venous return.

Here, we have Left Atrial Pressure vs. Pulmonary Venous Return. An increase in **transpulmonary pressure** (lung volume) increases lung volume, which tends to decrease the capacitance of these beds, squeezing blood into the pulmonary veins and the left atrium. But if the patient is volume deplete and pulmonary venous pressure is much lower than transpulmonary pressure (alveolar pressure minus pleural pressure), the increase in lung volume or transpulmonary pressure impairs venous return, showing an increased resistance effect.
