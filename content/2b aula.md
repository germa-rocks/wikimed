# Cardiovascular Physiology: Cardiac Function and Pressure-Volume Relationships

## Introduction
Now for the cardiac portion of cardiovascular physiology. Having discussed venous return and the venous return curve in Part 1, which was given to us by Dr. Guyton, we're now going to focus on the Frank-Starling cardiac function curve and a little bit more detailed analysis in terms of the Sagawa pressure-volume relationship. The difference between these curves and the relationships between them will become more clear as we fly through Part 2 of Chapter 2.

## The Cardiac Response Curve (Frank-Starling Curve)
We have only considered the circuit function (the venous return curve) that describes the relationship between right atrial pressure and venous return from the venous capacitance beds. But the cardiac response curve, or the cardiac function curve (also known as the Frank-Starling curve), relates right atrial pressure to cardiac output. It has an inverse relationship compared to the venous return curve. 

While the circuit function curve is shifted in response to variables like total volume, stressed and unstressed volume, compliance of the venous beds, and resistance to venous return, the cardiac response curve also shifts left and right in response to a number of variables. There seems to be this notion that the cardiac response curve shifts left and right just in response to cardiac contractility (e.g., giving adrenergic drugs shifts it up and left; beta-blockers shift it down and right). However, it actually just tells you the relationship between right atrial pressure and cardiac output. Since cardiac output is determined by heart rate and stroke volume, this curve will shift in response to changes in heart rate, as well as anything that changes stroke volume.

For a given right atrial pressure (which is a surrogate for stretch or a surrogate of volume, i.e., preload), you get a given blood flow or cardiac output. It is a "stupid" curve; it just tells you for a given stretch, what your output is. Changes in valve function, afterload, and contractility will all cause changes in the amount the heart can pump out. 

If you increase right atrial pressure, you get more output for the cardiac response curve. Conversely, for the venous return curve, if you lower right atrial pressure, you get more venous return. Comparing two right atrial pressures on the cardiac function curve, an increase in pressure leads to an increase in cardiac output. The mechanism behind this is increased myocyte stretch: as you stretch myocytes, the troponins become more sensitive to calcium (stress-induced calcium sensitivity), resulting in a greater contractile force. This is the Frank-Starling mechanism, known since the mid-1800s.

### Shifts in the Cardiac Function Curve
An upward and leftward shift represents an increase in cardiac function (similar to an increase in circuit function). For the same right atrial pressures, there is a greater cardiac output. A shift down and to the right does just the opposite: lower cardiac output and less response between the same two right atrial pressures.

A critical point that tethers heart-lung interaction is the x-intercept of this curve. If you clamp the SVC and IVC, stop the heart, and measure the pressure in the right atrium, that pressure will be equivalent to the intrathoracic pressure. Thus, if you arrest the circulation and uncouple venous return from the heart, the pressure in the right atrium becomes the intrathoracic pressure. This x-intercept is slightly sub-atmospheric (because intrathoracic pressure is slightly sub-atmospheric). As intrathoracic pressure changes with respiration, so too will this curve shift.

## The Sagawa Pressure-Volume Relationship
The cardiac function curve relates right atrial pressure to cardiac output as a gross method of analysis. It shifts not only due to contractility but also changes in diastolic properties, afterload, heart rate, and valve function. To prove this, we use a more detailed graphical analysis: the Sagawa pressure-volume relationship. It allows for a better understanding of how changes in diastolic and systolic properties of the heart affect global cardiac function, as well as an analysis of valvulopathies and afterload abnormalities.

### End-Diastolic and End-Systolic Pressure-Volume Relationships
The end-diastolic pressure-volume relationship (EDPVR) describes the ventricular diastolic properties and gives a qualitative understanding of ventricular filling. Looking at the left ventricle, the volume-pressure relationship during diastole looks like a hockey stick. Following a preceding systole at the end-systolic volume, the relaxed ventricle fills to the new end-diastolic volume. This gives you your end-diastolic volume and pressure.

Systole begins once the end-diastolic volume is reached. There is an abrupt increase in intraventricular pressure without a change in volume—this is isovolumic contraction. Once the diastolic pressure is reached in the aorta, the aortic valve opens, and ejection proceeds. Volume decreases and pressure increases as blood is ejected into the aorta. Finally, you reach the end-systolic pressure-volume relationship (ESPVR), which represents maximal systolic elastance during the cardiac cycle. The slope of the ESPVR is related to the contractile state of the heart. Once this is reached, relaxation begins (isovolumic relaxation), returning to the end-systolic volume, and diastole begins again.

The difference between end-diastolic volume and end-systolic volume is the **stroke volume**. Since cardiac output is stroke volume multiplied by heart rate, you can appreciate the relationship between this beat-to-beat graphical analysis and the bulk-flow Frank-Starling curve.

### Diastolic Function and Elastance
Elastance is the change in pressure in response to a change in volume (the inverse of compliance). In diastole, elastance is very low (high compliance), meaning the ventricle is distensible and stretchy, allowing large changes in volume with very small changes in pressure. 

Pure diastolic dysfunction represents an increase in elastance (a decrease in compliance). Graphically, compared to a normal patient, a patient with diastolic dysfunction will have a much higher filling pressure for the same end-diastolic volume. Causes include left ventricular hypertrophy (e.g., from poorly controlled hypertension) and restrictive cardiomyopathies. 

Importantly, volume loading of one ventricle can cause acute diastolic dysfunction in the opposite ventricle—this is known as **ventricular interdependence**. The relationship depends on the relative compliances of the septum versus the free walls. If you volume load the right ventricle (e.g., during an acute pulmonary embolism), you can cause acute diastolic dysfunction in the left ventricle. For example, a patient with an acute PE might have their pulmonary capillary wedge pressure double due to an abrupt increase in RV afterload and RV volume, which pushes the interventricular septum into the LV, altering the LV's end-diastolic pressure-volume relationship. This higher filling pressure to achieve the same or lower stroke volume represents an impaired cardiac function curve (a downward and rightward shift).

### Systolic Function
The ESPVR gives a qualitative understanding of the contractile state of the left ventricle. This is the systolic elastance curve. In systole, there is a very high elastance (low compliance). A small change in volume results in a large change in pressure. 

Pure systolic dysfunction represents a shift down and to the right of the ESPVR (a true impairment in the contractile state, such as from a beta-blocker overdose or dilated cardiomyopathy with a low ejection fraction). This results in a higher filling pressure needed to achieve the same (or lower) stroke volume. Like diastolic dysfunction, pure systolic dysfunction also results in an impaired cardiac function curve.

## Right Ventricle vs. Left Ventricle PV Loops
The pressure-volume relationship was derived heavily from left ventricular physiology, but there are qualitative and quantitative differences between the right and left ventricles. 

Qualitatively, the right ventricular isovolumic contraction is much attenuated compared to the left ventricle. Ejection in the right ventricle actually continues beyond the termination of left ventricular systole. This happens because the afterload of the RV (the pulmonary vascular bed) is much lower, highly compliant, and distensible compared to the systemic bed. As a result, the pulmonary valve stays open longer, and right ventricular volume continues to decrease even beyond peak systolic elastance. The right ventricle lacks a true isovolumic relaxation phase because it slurs out, continuing to eject due to the highly compliant bed. 

Quantitatively, the left ventricle generates much higher pressures (systolic pressures well above 100 mmHg), whereas the normal right ventricle stays below 30 mmHg. The normal RV PV loop is somewhat triangular, whereas the LV PV loop is more rectangular. However, in the setting of chronic pulmonary hypertension, the right ventricle hypertrophies and its PV loop begins to resemble the left ventricle's rectangular shape.

## Ventricular-Vascular Coupling and Arterial Elastance
The elastance of the ventricle is not an on/off state; it lies on a continuum between full relaxation and contraction, known as time-varying elastance. The elastance of the aorta and pulmonary artery is also changing. The superposition of time-varying elastance of the ventricle and the artery yields a concept known as ventricular-vascular coupling.

Arterial elastance is a composite variable that best represents ventricular afterload. It can be approximated graphically by a line connecting the end-diastolic volume on the X-axis to the end-systolic pressure-volume point. The slope of this line defines the arterial impedance into which the ventricle ejects. 

If a patient with normal systolic and diastolic function faces poor arterial compliance (e.g., an elderly patient with stiff vessels), the arterial elastance curve shifts upwards and to the left. The ventricle ejects into a highly inelastic bed, hitting the end-systolic pressure-volume relationship at a much lower volume and higher pressure, resulting in a reduced stroke volume. This alone can cause an impaired cardiac function curve. Thus, an isolated change in afterload (arterial impedance) can shift the cardiac function curve down and to the right, even with normal contractility.

## Vascular Impedance vs. Resistance
Arterial impedance is a more physiological measure of afterload than classical resistance. Resistance follows Poiseuille's law (inversely proportional to the radius to the fourth power) and assumes rigid, non-distensible tubes. However, arteries are distensible. Impedance takes into account the geometric summation of both classical resistance and "reactance" (vascular distensibility). 

The distensibility of an artery creates the "Windkessel effect," where the artery expands during systole and recoils during diastole, maintaining continuous flow. Decreased vascular compliance increases reactance, which increases overall impedance. This is why a patient with stiff arteries can suffer from heart failure even if their classical vascular resistance isn't excessively high.

## Valvulopathies and Pressure-Volume Loops

### Mitral Regurgitation
In mitral regurgitation, the effective ejection fraction decreases. The left ventricle no longer has an isovolumic contraction phase because blood immediately ejects backwards into the low-pressure left atrium as soon as ventricular pressure exceeds left atrial pressure. The total stroke volume increases (because it's ejecting into a low-resistance pathway), but the *forward* stroke volume decreases. Graphically, the PV loop loses its vertical right edge (isovolumic contraction) and becomes rounded. Treatment focuses on reducing afterload (reducing arterial impedance) so that more blood goes forward into the aorta rather than backward into the atrium.

### Aortic Stenosis
Aortic stenosis represents a very high arterial impedance to flow due to a fixed stenotic lesion. The left ventricle must generate exceptionally high pressures (sometimes 200-250 mmHg) to overcome the obstruction. The PV loop becomes very tall and narrow, resulting in a low stroke volume. Over time, the ventricle thickens (hypertrophy), leading to a shift of the end-diastolic pressure-volume curve upward and to the left. This means the patient develops severe diastolic dysfunction on top of the pressure overload. They become highly dependent on preload to maintain stroke volume, making them very sensitive to drops in volume.

## Pulmonary Vascular Resistance (PVR)
There are many misconceptions about pulmonary vascular resistance. PVR is often treated as a constant number, assuming a linear relationship between pressure and flow. However, the pulmonary vascular bed is highly distensible and not composed of rigid pipes. 

As you apply a driving pressure, flow begins. As you continue to increase pressure, flow increases exponentially. This happens because of two phenomena:
1. **Recruitment**: Previously collapsed pulmonary capillaries open up.
2. **Distension**: Already open capillaries stretch and increase their radius.

Because resistance is inversely proportional to the radius to the fourth power, as the vessels distend, resistance drops dramatically. Therefore, the relationship between pressure and flow is non-linear. Calculating a single static number for PVR can give false positives, especially during dynamic changes like exercise or fluid loading. 

### PEEP, Transpulmonary Pressure, and Hypoxemia
The application of Positive End-Expiratory Pressure (PEEP) or an increase in transpulmonary pressure (like during mechanical ventilation) affects PVR. The relationship between lung volume and PVR is U-shaped. At very low lung volumes, extra-alveolar vessels are compressed, increasing resistance. At very high lung volumes, the alveoli overdistend and crush the alveolar capillaries, also increasing resistance. The lowest PVR is near the Functional Residual Capacity (FRC). 

Adding hypoxemia to this system causes hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction. This shifts the pressure-flow curve to the right, meaning for the same driving pressure, there is much less blood flow, drastically increasing true pulmonary vascular resistance.

## Conclusion
This concludes the integration of cardiovascular physiology, taking us from the venous return curves to the Frank-Starling mechanism, into the detailed Sagawa pressure-volume relationships, and finishing with ventricular-vascular coupling and pulmonary vascular resistance. Understanding these curves and their dynamic shifts is essential for mastering cardiorespiratory interactions and managing critically ill patients.
